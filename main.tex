\begin{abstract}


In \textit{out-of-domain} (OOD) sound-matching, a synthesizer is optimized to mimic a sound it cannot accurately replicate. Systematic OOD evaluation is underexplored in part because the standard evaluation metric of ``parameter loss'' requires ground-truth parameters that do not exist in OOD settings. We address this by pairing synthesizers that share some but not all functionality (such as band-pass filters applied to different source signals) so that the shared parameters (e.g., filter cutoffs) can be compared automatically. We call this Partial Parameter Loss: the distance between the critical parameters that both synthesizers have in common.

We apply this methodology across 7 scenarios involving band-pass filtering, amplitude modulation, and pitch-bending synthesizers which were evaluated with 4 differentiable loss functions (\SIMSESpec, \LoneSpec, \JTFS, \DTWEnv), then validated against blinded human listening tests. Our results confirm that loss function effectiveness remains tightly coupled to the method of synthesis in OOD settings: \SIMSESpec excels at filter cutoff recovery, \DTWEnv consistently recovers amplitude modulation parameters, and \JTFS dominates smooth pitch trajectories. Parameter-based evaluation agrees with hearing tests on the top-ranked loss function in 5 of 7 scenarios, demonstrating utility as a diagnostic tool for identifying loss function capabilities. These findings provide both empirical guidance for selecting similarity measures in practical sound-matching applications and a replicable framework for future OOD experimentation.

\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

% Sound-matching — the automatic programming of a synthesizer to approximate a target sound — has been studied for decades [4, 5, 6], yet nearly all systematic evaluations assume an in-domain setting where the target originates from the same synthesizer being optimized [5, 7, 8, 9, 10]. In practice, the sounds that designers wish to recreate come from different instruments, recordings, or synthesis engines, making exact replication impossible. This out-of-domain (OOD) setting better reflects real-world sound design [1, 3], but it introduces a fundamental evaluation problem: the standard metric of parameter loss requires ground-truth parameters that do not exist when the target and imitator synthesizers differ. The lack of reliable automatic evaluation methods is a major hurdle in OOD sound-matching, and has contributed to OOD experiments being largely avoided in the literature.

% Digital audio synthesizers have become ubiquitous in music production and sound design~\cite{steinberg1996vst}. A synthesizer consists of a series of digital signal processing (DSP) functions that transform a set of control parameters into audio outputs~\cite{stranneby2004digital}. 

In sound design, artists iteratively manipulate the parameters of their digital synthesizers to produce a desired sound~\cite{krekovic2019insights}. While this manual process can be a rewarding creative outlet, it often requires expertise, patience, and significant time investment. \textit{Sound-matching} refers to the automation of this workflow: given a target sound and a synthesizer, the objective is to identify parameters that produce a perceptually similar output~\cite{justice1979analytic,horner1993machine,mitchell2007evolutionary}. Sound-matching experiments may be conducted in \textit{in-domain} or \textit{out-of-domain} (OOD) settings. For in-domain experiments, the target sound and outputs which mimic the target are generated by the same synthesizer. In OOD settings, the target sound originates from a different synthesis process or recording, and exact replication is impossible. OOD sound-matching shares a much closer resemblance to practical sound-design, yet prior work overwhelmingly focuses on in-domain evaluations~\cite{horner1993machine,yee2018automatic,han2023perceptual,vahidi2023mesostructures,salimi2025evaluating}. Since manual audio evaluations are impractical at scale, the lack of reliable automatic evaluation methods is a major hurdle in OOD sound-matching.

We propose an approach to OOD experiments that can be automatically evaluated, and use this methodology to test takeaways from recent in-domain experiments. In particular, \anon{Salimi et al.}~\cite{salimi2025evaluating} tested 4 loss functions in different in-domain sound-matching tasks, and observed that there is no best universal loss function; that is, the performance of loss functions depends on the method of synthesis and the needs of the sound designer. Here, we want to test whether the previously observed strengths and weaknesses of loss functions also occur in OOD scenarios; therefore, we design synthesizers that cannot make identical outputs, use one to generate a target sound, and modify the parameters of the other to mimic the target. The mismatch between the synthesizers must be such that there is a partial, critical overlap between the parameters. For example, if one synthesizer is the amplitude modulation of white noise, and one synthesizer is the amplitude modulation of a sine wave, then the rate of amplitude modulation is the partially overlapping parameter, and matching the modulation rate is the most reasonable measure of success. We call the application of parameter loss to mismatched synthesizers \textit{partial parameter loss} (PPL).

 \textit{The central hypothesis of this work is that the performance rankings of loss functions vary based on the synthesizers used in OOD scenarios.} The research questions addressed in this work are: \textbf{(RQ1)} Does synthesis-dependent performance of loss functions (as observed for in-domain settings) also occur in OOD? \textbf{(RQ2)} Do the reported advantages and limitations of common loss functions persist under partial synthesizer mismatch? \textbf{(RQ3)} Can PPL provide sufficient automatic evaluation to enable controlled OOD experiments, and how well do its results align with human perception?


\textbf{Contributions:} 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We demonstrate that loss-function effectiveness depends on the methods of synthesis in OOD settings, confirming that this pattern is not isolated to in-domain evaluation. Different similarity measures excel in filtering, amplitude-modulation, and pitch-modulation scenarios. This finding has direct practical consequences: the guidance developed from in-domain experiments transfers to OOD settings, and loss function selection should be informed by the synthesis task at hand.

    \item  We propose a methodology for conducting controlled OOD sound-matching experiments by designing synthesizer pairs with partially overlapping functionality. This provides the first systematic framework for OOD evaluation in sound-matching, addressing a gap where OOD experiments have been largely avoided despite their importance for verifying loss function performance.
    
    \item We introduce Partial Parameter Loss (PPL) as an automated evaluation metric. PPL measures task-specific loss function performance (i.e., whether a loss function recovers the parameters of interest) rather than replacing holistic perceptual evaluation. We validate PPL against blinded human listening tests and show its utility as a diagnostic tool for identifying loss function capabilities. Our findings suggest that PPL can be a more appropriate evaluation measure than P-Loss for in-domain scenarios, particularly when direct replication is not the main goal. 
\end{enumerate}

Together, these contributions address significant gaps in the sound-matching literature, where OOD experiments have been largely avoided despite their critical importance. By providing both an evaluation methodology and empirical evidence that synthesis-dependent behavior persists under OOD conditions, this work offers practical sound-matching suggestions and a framework for future OOD research. \section{Background and Previous Work}

\subsection{Measuring Audio Similarity}
\label{sec:measuring_audio_similarity}

Spectral measurements of audio similarity are the go-to functions for representation and comparison of sounds~\cite{smith2007mathematics}. Fourier transformations extract the strength of frequencies present in a signal --- the range and resolution of frequencies detected is dependent on the sampling rate and length of the signal. \textit{Short-time Fourier Transform} (STFT) is perhaps the most common application, where Fourier transformation is applied to segments of the signal~\cite{smith2007mathematics}. Despite their ubiquity, there are many documented issues with the application of distance metrics on Fourier-based losses~\cite{turian2020sorry,salimi2025evaluating,vahidi2023mesostructures}.

\textit{Joint Time-Frequency Scattering} (JTFS) is the application of wavelet transformations to a time-frequency representation of sounds~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures,anden2015joint}. It has been recently proposed as an improvement to Fourier-based losses when measuring \textit{mesostructures} of sounds --- that is, anything from microstructures of waveshapes to macrostructures of musical arrangements~\cite{lostanlen2019shape}. However, the capabilities of this function are likely to be much narrower than speculated~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}, an idea which we further test in this work.

\textit{Parameter Loss} (P-Loss) is another important measure of audio similarity~\cite{yee2018automatic,esling2019flow}. P-Loss requires that the synthesizers making the two sounds have identical parameters. It is computed as the distance between normalized parameter vectors,  or $\text{P-Loss} = \|\theta - \theta_0\|_1$. It must be noted that despite having the term \textit{loss} in its name, P-Loss is generally used as a final evaluation measure of a sound-matching, not as a loss function that guides the search process. This distinction between loss and evaluation measure is further discussed in the next section.

\subsection{Sound-Matching Formulation}
\label{sec:sound_matching_formulation}


Sound-matching is formulated as optimizing parameters $\theta$ of a synthesizer $g(\theta)$ such that $x_\theta = g(\theta)$ matches a target $x^t = g^t(\theta^t)$. In OOD settings, $g$ and $g^t$ differ such that their output sets do not overlap. Similarity is measured with a loss function $L$ that compares the two sounds, and (assuming a differentiable environment) $\theta$ is iteratively updated via gradients of $L$ until convergence.


Sound-matching experiments are either \emph{in-domain} or \emph{out-of-domain} (OOD). For in-domain experiments, the target sound is generated by the same synthesizer being optimized ($g = g^{t}$), ground-truth parameters are known, and exact replication is achievable. In OOD experiments, the target originates from a different synthesizer or recording, for example, imitating a violin sound with vocals. OOD settings introduce challenges that are absent with in-domain settings: parameter ground truth does not exist (making standard P-Loss undefined), the imitator (by definition) lacks the ability to fully reproduce all features of the target, and there is a degree of subjectivity in determining which features should be imitated and how success should be measured.

OOD experiments have often either been skipped entirely, or retroactively used for finetuning of existing models~\cite{masuda2021soundmatch,uzrad2024diffmoog}. Existing OOD studies are limited in scope and do not systematically compare loss functions across synthesis methods~\cite{creativecherep2024,salimi2025evaluating}. Here we are particularly interested in the takeaways from a recent work by \anon{Salimi et al.}, which measures the performance of 4 loss functions across different in-domain sound-matching tasks~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}. In this in-domain work, \anon{Salimi et al.} argue that a universal loss function for audio is unlikely to exist, and the selection of a loss function is dependent on the sound-designer as well as the method of synthesis~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}. Here we conduct a number of experiments to test whether the conclusions made by \anon{Salimi et al.} extends to OOD settings.

Here we test whether these conclusions extend to OOD settings.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
\subsection{Problem Setup}
We evaluate 4 differentiable loss functions across 5 out-of-domain (OOD) and 2 in-domain sound-matching scenarios. The experiment structure is as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{scenario} is defined by a pair of synthesizers --- an imitator $g$ and a target $g^t$. Each scenario represents a distinct sound-design task.
    \item Within each scenario, 4 \textbf{experiments} are conducted, one for each loss function.
    \item Each experiment consists of \textbf{300 independent trials}. In each trial, a target sound is generated by randomly sampling parameters $\theta^t$ for $g^t$, the imitator parameters $\hat{\theta}$ are randomly initialized, and 200 steps of gradient-based optimization are performed to minimize the loss between $g(\hat{\theta})$ and $x^t = g^t(\theta^t)$.
    \item Each trial is evaluated and given a performance score.
\end{enumerate}

This yields 300 automatically assigned trial scores per loss function per scenario, enabling robust statistical comparison. Additionally, hearing tests are conducted for verification of results. All synthesizers produce 1-second audio signals at a sampling rate of 48{,}000~Hz.



\subsection{Loss Functions}
\label{subsec:loss_functions}
In Section~\ref{sec:measuring_audio_similarity}, the 4 loss functions used in \anon{a recent in-domain study} by \anon{Salimi et al.} were discussed~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}. The same loss functions are tested in this work, as we are interested in their strengths and weaknesses and how they interact with different synthesizers in an OOD setting. Here we provide a brief summary of the loss functions as described by \anon{Salimi et al.}

\LoneSpec is the pointwise L1 distance between log-magnitude STFT spectrograms, and \SIMSESpec utilizes the SIMSE instead of L1. For \JTFS loss, the L2 distance metric is applied to the 1-Dimensional JTFS transform~\cite{kymatio}. \DTWEnv calculates the soft-DTW  distance~\cite{softdtw_jax,cuturi2017soft} between amplitude envelopes. The envelope of the sound is calculated by summing the values of all frequency bins at every time-step of an STFT transformation. For all STFT-based computations (\LoneSpec, \SIMSESpec, and the envelope extraction for \DTWEnv), we use 512 FFT bins, a window size of 600 samples, and a hop length of 100 samples, consistent with previous work~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}.


\subsection{Evaluation Methods}
\label{subsec:evaluation}
The methods used for evaluation of our experiments are discussed here. These are the automatic Partial P-Loss (PPL), and blinded human listening tests. The procedures for statistical comparison of the resulting scores are described in Section~\ref{sec:bootstrapping_ranking}.


\subsubsection{Partial P-Loss}
\label{sec:partial_ploss}
 P-Loss measures the distance between the full parameter vectors of the target and output synthesizers~\cite{yee2018automatic}. In OOD settings, the target and imitator synthesizers have different architectures or parameter ranges, making full parameter comparison impossible. We reiterate an example given earlier in the introduction: assume that the target sound is a sine wave at frequency 200 Hz with amplitude modulation applied at frequency of 10 Hz. The sound-designer wants to imitate this sound using a synthesizer that generates saw waves at frequency parameter ranges of 1100-1300 Hz and amplitude modulation parameter range of 0-30 Hz. In this OOD case, the only reasonable matching possibility is the amplitude modulation rate. \textit{Partial Parameter Loss} (PPL) is similar to P-Loss; however, it only measures the distance across the subset of parameters that have critical perceptual correspondence between the two synthesizers.

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{PPL} = \| \theta^{(\mathrm{crit})}_t - \hat{\theta}^{(\mathrm{crit})} \|_2
\end{equation}
$\theta^{(\mathrm{crit})}_t$ and $\hat{\theta}^{(\mathrm{crit})}$ denote the corresponding parameters of interest between $g$ and $g^t$.  Critical parameters are normalized in the range of [0,1].

 Here, we hypothesize that PPL can be an effective method of automatic evaluation for sound-matching using mismatched synthesizers. The expectation of domain knowledge and some level of subjectivity is unavoidable in selecting such critical parameters. However, the scenarios that we propose in Section~\ref{sec:scenarios} have been designed with a focus on a single synthesis task in order to make the PPL definition fairly straightforward. With careful design, PPL can be a reasonable interpretable comparison across synthesizers. The exact PPL parameters used for each scenario will be discussed in their corresponding subsection later on in Section~\ref{sec:scenarios}.

\subsubsection{Hearing tests}
Listening tests serve as the primary ground truth for verification of success in sound-matching. We closely follow the methodology of \anon{Salimi et al.}~\cite{salimi2020make}. In each scenario we randomly sample 40 sound-pairs (target and imitator output) for each of the 4 loss functions; 4 of the authors independently evaluated these 160 sound-pairs in a blinded manner, not knowing which loss function was used. The listeners answered the question "are the two sounds similar?" by assigning a Likert score from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree) for each sound-pair. As discussed later in Section~\ref{sec:summary_of_results}, an overall moderate intraclass correlation~\cite{liljequist2019intraclass} was observed in the ratings of all authors. The scores from all listeners were combined, resulting in 160 evaluation scores per loss function per scenario. 

\subsection{Optimization Loop}
\label{subsec:optimization}
The optimization loop is a differentiable process of generating sounds using a synthesizer, comparing it to a target using loss function, and updating the synthesizer's parameters to minimize the loss. Following the methodology of previous work~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}, the 4 loss functions are defined as differentiable JAX functions~\cite{deepmind2020jax}. The synthesizers are implemented in Faust and transpiled into JAX using DawDreamer~\cite{braun2024dac}.  This pipeline is faster than real-time and parallelizable, and allows description of synthesizers in a convenient DSP language. In each trial, the imitator synthesizer parameters $\hat{\theta}$ are initialized by uniform random sampling over the parameter ranges defined in the Faust program. The target parameters $\theta^t$ are similarly sampled. Parameters are updated using the RMSProp optimizer with a fixed learning rate of 0.045, and maximum number of iterations is set to 200, consistent with previous work~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}. 

Following the methodology of previous work~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}, gradient clipping is applied to mitigate exploding gradients. Furthermore, recent work has shown that gradient clipping can improve neural network optimization for perceptual sound-matching~\cite{han2025gradient}. 

\subsection{Bootstrapping and Ranking Procedures}
\label{sec:bootstrapping_ranking}
We follow the methodology of \anon{Salimi et al.}~\cite{salimi2025evaluating} for ranking the loss functions. For each loss function and scenario, we bootstrap 1,000 resampled means from the 300 PPL scores (or 160 Likert scores) to obtain nonparametric performance distributions~\cite{chernick2011bootstrap}. These distributions are then ranked using the \textit{nonparametric Scott-Knott procedure} (NPSK), which recursively partitions distributions into statistically distinct groups without assuming normality~\cite{tantithamthavorn2017mvt,tantithamthavorn2018optimization}. Rank 1 indicates the best-performing loss; ties indicate distributions that NPSK cannot distinguish. The scenario rankings are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:scenario_ranks}, and we will discuss each scenario and the rankings in the following sections.

\subsection{AM-Synthesizer Matching}
\label{sec:am_sound_matching}

 This group contains three OOD scenarios built from two AM synthesizers.  \FMMod and \FMModvtwo, as described in Listing~\ref{lst:program3}, are the basis for the three variations of this scenario. \FMMod generates a sound by modifying the amplitude of a saw oscillator with a sinusoidal LFO. \FMModvtwo uses sine oscillators for both the carrier and the modulator. Parameters \texttt{amp} and \texttt{car} determine the LFO and carrier frequencies. The value of \texttt{amp} shapes the amplitude of the sound (or the wobbling effect), and \texttt{car} determines the frequency (or pitch) of the sound.

In these scenarios, \texttt{amp} values can match, but the harmonic content (or \texttt{car}) cannot. In \textit{Non-Overlapping Frequencies}, the available frequency ranges are different. In \textit{ Sine Target$\rightarrow$Saw Imitator}, the target can only make sine tones while the imitator can only make saw waves, and vice versa for Saw Target$\rightarrow$Sine Imitator. 

%A question we have to consider is: how would we match the sounds manually? Here we see why OOD experiments are difficult, as describing sound similarity can be quite subjective: matching of the modulate rate is a very reasonable goal, but can't straightforwardly answer whether a saw wave at 50 or 75 Hz is closer to a saw wave at 100 Hz; while 75 Hz is closer in an absolute sense, 50 Hz is more harmonically similar to 100 Hz~\cite{sethares2005tuning,young1939terminology}.


\noindent \textbf{PPL:} Across all three AM variants, PPL is defined as the squared difference between the \texttt{amp} values of the target and imitator. The \texttt{amp}  parameter is meaningful across synthesizers because the amplitude modulation rate transfers perceptually regardless of the carrier waveform or frequency range. In these scenarios, carrier harmonic content cannot match exactly, so the ``correct'' frequency is ambiguous and excluded as a critical parameter.


% \textbf{Why exclude the carrier frequency from PPL?} In OOD settings where the carrier frequencies cannot match exactly, the ``correct'' frequency is ambiguous. Consider a target producing 440 Hz (A4 in equal temperament) and an imitator limited to frequencies below 400 Hz. The closest numerical value is 400 Hz, but musically, 220 Hz (A3, one octave below) may be a better perceptual match than 392 Hz (G4, a different note). More generally, given the logarithmic nature of pitch perception, the best frequency matches for a given frequency $f$ are arguably $f \cdot 2^n$ for integer $n$~\cite{young1939terminology,sethares2005tuning}. Because there is no single correct definition of carrier frequency similarity in the OOD setting, we exclude it from PPL and focus on the amplitude modulation rate, which is both unambiguous and a common transfer target in practical sound design~\cite{engel2020ddsp}.


\subsubsection{Non-Overlapping Frequencies}
\label{sec:am_sound_matching_nonoverlapping}
\textbf{Setup:} Two instances of \FMModvtwo with non-overlapping carrier ranges: 30--250~Hz (target) and 1000--5000~Hz (imitator). Since both use sine carriers, harmonic clashing is factored out; the tone can never match, making this an OOD scenario.

\noindent \textbf{Results:}
\DTWEnv is the best performer under both PPL and hearing tests, consistent with its focus on amplitude envelope dynamics: since the carrier frequencies cannot match, the modulation rate is the only transferable feature.

\subsubsection{Overlapping Frequencies (Sine $\leftrightarrow$ Saw)}
\label{sec:am_sine_saw}
\textbf{Setup:} We run two mirrored scenarios using \FMModvtwo and \FMMod, both with \texttt{amp} range of 1--15~Hz and \texttt{car} range of 30--5000~Hz. In one, the sine-based synthesizer is the target and the saw-based synthesizer is the imitator; in the other, the roles are reversed. Unlike the non-overlapping scenario, carrier frequencies can overlap numerically, but harmonic content still differs (pure tone vs.\ harmonic series).

\noindent \textbf{Results:} In both scenarios, \DTWEnv achieves the best PPL score, confirming its ability to recover the correct modulation rate. However, hearing tests favor spectrogram-based losses (\SIMSESpec in one direction, \LoneSpec in the other). The likely explanation is that \DTWEnv matches the amplitude modulation accurately but ignores harmonic content, while spectrogram losses produce outputs with better overall timbral similarity at the cost of envelope accuracy. This highlights the divergence between PPL (which measures a specific parameter) and holistic perceptual judgments.
\subsection{Pitch-Bending}
\label{sec:chirplet_scenarios}
This group tests three scenarios involving synthesizers that produce tones with increasing pitch, or ``chirplets''~\cite{mann1995chirplet,lostanlen2019shape}. The main scenario (Section 4.3.1) is OOD, while the remaining two (Sections 4.3.2 and 4.3.3) are in-domain variants included for comparison with prior work by Vahidi et al.\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures}. 

The base synthesizer (Listing~\ref{lst:pitchbendup}) produces a sine wave at a starting pitch that remains steady for a delay period, after which an exponential upward pitch bend is applied. The search parameters are the starting pitch (30–500 Hz) and the rate of pitch increase (1–20 samples per time step).


\subsubsection{Delayed Pitch-Bending}
\label{sec:delayed_chirplet}
\textbf{Setup:} The program here is a sine wave with a steady starting pitch for a random amount of time; after a time delay of 5000-30000 samples, an exponential upward pitch bend is applied to the starting pitch. The time-delay for the pitch-bending onset is selected randomly for each experiment, so it is very unlikely that the imitator would be able to replicate the target sounds, making this an OOD scenario. 

\noindent \textbf{PPL:} Success is evaluated by how well the \texttt{increase\_speed} and \texttt{starting\_pitch} parameters are approximated despite the delay in the pitch-bending onset, using L2 distance. The delay parameter is not adjusted during search, therefore not part of the evaluation.

\noindent \textbf{Results:} This scenario shows identical ratings between both PPL and hearing tests. Both measures show \JTFS as the best performing loss. This is consistent with the findings by Vahidi et al.~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures} which showed \JTFS to be agnostic to slight perturbations in the frequency and time shifts when matching chirplet synthesizers. 

\subsubsection{No-Delay Pitch-Bending}
\label{sec:no_delayed_chirplet}
\textbf{Setup:} As a follow-up experiment, we conducted the \textit{in-domain} comparison of pitch-bending synthesizers \textit{without} delay. This scenario would be a close replication of the original in-domain experiments by Vahidi \textit{et al.}~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures}. In other words, it is identical to the delayed pitch-bending experiment but the delay variable $\delta$ is always set to 0 (see Listing~\ref{lst:pitchbendup}).  

\noindent \textbf{Results:} \JTFS remains the best performer under both PPL and hearing tests, consistent with the OOD delayed scenario and with prior work~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures}. 

\subsubsection{Pulsating Pitch-Bending}
\label{sec:pulsating_chirplet}
\textbf{Setup:} This scenario modifies the pitch-bending synthesizer to include a pulsating component (Listing~\ref{lst:pitchbendup_pulse}). The frequency increases overall but also oscillates up and down within a narrow range, driven by a saw-wave LFO with frequency \texttt{pulse\_rate}. The search parameters are \texttt{increase\_speed} and \texttt{pulse\_rate}. This is an in-domain experiment included to test whether \JTFS's advantage extends to pitch trajectories with added modulation --- a feature that falls within the definition of ``mesostructure'' as described by Vahidi et al.~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures}. PPL is defined as the L2 distance between the normalized \texttt{increase\_speed} and \texttt{pulse\_rate} parameters.

\noindent \textbf{Results:} \DTWEnv ranks first under both PPL and hearing tests, displacing \JTFS from the top rank it held in the other two chirplet scenarios. \JTFS drops to second place. This result is significant because the pulsating pitch trajectory falls under the definition of mesostructure and represents only a slight modification to the original chirplet experiments. This suggests that \JTFS's strength is more narrowly scoped than the ``mesostructure'' framing implies.


\subsection{Key Findings}
\label{sec:summary_of_results}
Table~\ref{tab:scenario_ranks} presents the full ranking results across all seven scenarios. A few patterns can be observed:

No loss function consistently ranks above the others. \SIMSESpec{} is the top performer for band-pass matching, \DTWEnv{} for amplitude modulation and pulsating pitch-bending, and \JTFS{} for chirplet-like pitch trajectories. Parameter-based evaluations assign identical top ranks in five of seven scenarios. The two disagreements occur in the AM scenarios with overlapping carrier frequencies (Section~\ref{sec:am_sine_saw}), where PPL favors \DTWEnv{} for its accurate recovery of the modulation rate while listeners favor spectrogram-based losses for their better overall timbral similarity.

\LoneSpec{}, despite being a commonly used spectrogram loss in the literature, ranks 1 in only one instance (Likert score for \textbf{AM: saw $\rightarrow$ sine}). Scale-invariant losses such as \SIMSESpec{} may be an effective alternative to spectrogram-based loss computation.

Finally, we used intraclass correlation (ICC) to measure inter-rater reliability of blinded hearing test results of the 4 authors~\cite{liljequist2019intraclass}. Specifically, we used the ICC3 formula, which prioritizes the relative rankings and not the exact scores given by each listener. The overall coefficient was 0.61 with a near-zero p-value; this moderate overall agreement between listeners strengthens the use of hearing tests as the ground truth. The top row of Table~\ref{tab:scenario_ranks} shows the per-program ICC3 values.

\section{Discussion and Implications}

The results in Table~\ref{tab:scenario_ranks} confirm that synthesis-dependent loss behavior generalizes to OOD settings \textbf{(RQ1)}, and that the reported advantages and limitations of each loss function largely persist under synthesizer mismatch \textbf{(RQ2)}, with one important caveat: in AM scenarios with overlapping carriers, \DTWEnv achieves the best PPL but is outperformed perceptually by spectrogram losses, revealing a trade-off between matching specific features and overall timbre. PPL reliably identifies loss function capabilities when the critical parameters capture the perceptually dominant feature, but should be complemented by listening tests when multiple perceptual factors compete \textbf{(RQ3)}.

A notable result is that \JTFS excels for smooth pitch trajectories but underperforms on pulsating pitch-bends, where \DTWEnv dominates. The pulsating trajectory experiments support doubts expressed in \anon{previous work}\cite{salimi2025evaluating} regarding \JTFS, showing that its appropriate domain is much more narrowly scoped than ``mesostructures'' as a whole~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures}.

\noindent \textbf{Practical Implications:} Choice of loss function should be guided by the synthesis task: spectrogram-based losses (preferably \SIMSESpec or \LoneSpec) for filtering, \DTWEnv for amplitude modulation, and \JTFS for smooth pitch trajectories. SIMSE is a viable alternative to L1 and L2 for spectrogram comparisons; notably, \LoneSpec never ranked first under hearing tests despite being a commonly used spectrogram loss. For OOD benchmarks, PPL with overlapping synthesizers is a viable evaluation strategy, provided the critical parameters are perceptually dominant; validating against a smaller set of listening tests is recommended. The utility of PPL may also extend to in-domain scenarios, where the main goal is not direct replication of the parameters, but imitation of specific sound characteristics.

% \textbf{Specialized losses can outperform general-purpose metrics.} Both \DTWEnv{} and \SIMSESpec{} outperform more established approaches such as \JTFS{} and \LoneSpec{} when applied to tasks aligned with their design. This aligns with the takeaway from the in-domain experiments of past work~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}, suggesting that developing additional loss functions targeting specific perceptual dimensions (e.g., roughness, spectral centroid trajectories, or onset characteristics) may be more productive than pursuing a single universal metric. 


\section{Conclusion, Limitations, and Future Work}

\textbf{Summary:}  This work proposed a methodology for controlled OOD sound-matching experiments based on synthesizer pairs with partially overlapping functionality, and introduced PPL as an automatic evaluation metric within this framework. We used this OOD framework for testing recent in-domain observations~\cite{salimi2025evaluating,vahidi2023mesostructures}. Across multiple OOD scenarios, loss function effectiveness remains coupled to the method of synthesis, consistent with in-domain findings. PPL agreed with hearing tests on the top-ranked loss in 3 of 5 (excluding the two in-domain experiments) scenarios; disagreements occurred predictably when perceptually important features fell outside the critical parameter set, which also led to lower ICC3 values. PPL proved useful as a diagnostic tool for verifying loss function capabilities, such as the limitations of \JTFS and the strengths of \DTWEnv. PPL may also be a useful alternative to P-Loss in some in-domain scenarios.

\noindent \textbf{Weaknesses:} Whether PPL remains a useful metric for more complex synthesizers with higher-dimensional parameter spaces is an open question. Furthermore, the definition of ``critical parameters'' in PPL requires domain knowledge and judgment, introducing a degree of subjectivity. Additionally, the target sounds here originate from other synthesizers rather than from real-world recordings, which would introduce further complexity beyond the reach of simple DSP models.

\noindent  \textbf{Future Work:} Sound-matching is not practical until successful OOD evaluations with pre-recorded targets (regardless of source) are achieved, and this work is intended as a starting point. Defining novel loss functions and combining their strengths is an interesting path forward. Use of heuristics such as adaptive optimizers or reinforcement learning~\cite{sutton1998reinforcement} would likely be an improvement over fixed-rate gradient descent, and more effective at navigating loss function landscapes. Real-time sound-matching experiments are another important yet rarely explored requirement for practical sound-matching applications~\cite{shier2024real}.
