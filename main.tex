\begin{abstract}


Out-of-domain (OOD) sound-matching is the task of automatically programming a synthesizer toward a sound that it cannot accurately replicate. Despite its practical relevance, OOD sound-matching has rarely been explored, and systematic OOD evaluation is virtually absent from the literature. This gap persists in part because standard evaluation metrics such as ``parameter loss'' are not applicable when ground-truth parameters do not exist, and because no established methodology exists for designing controlled OOD experiments. 

We address this gap by proposing a methodology for conducting controlled OOD experiments. Synthesizer pairs are designed to partially overlap in functionality, and the overlapping parameters serve as a partial form of parameter loss (PPL), enabling automatic evaluation when full parameter ground truth does not exist. This methodology allows us to test whether the synthesis-dependent loss behavior observed in in-domain settings persists under OOD conditions.

We apply this methodology across seven scenarios involving band-pass filtering, amplitude modulation, and pitch-bending synthesizers, evaluated with four differentiable loss functions (\SIMSESpec, \LoneSpec, \JTFS, \DTWEnv) and validated against blinded human listening tests. Our results confirm that loss function effectiveness remains tightly coupled to the method of synthesis in OOD settings: \SIMSESpec excels at filter cutoff recovery, \DTWEnv consistently recovers amplitude modulation parameters, and \JTFS dominates smooth pitch trajectories. Parameter-based losses agrees with hearing tests on the top-ranked loss in five of seven scenarios, demonstrating utility as a diagnostic tool for identifying loss function capabilities. These findings provide both empirical guidance for selecting similarity measures in practical sound-matching applications and a replicable framework for future OOD experimentation.

\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}


Digital audio synthesizers have become ubiquitous in music production and sound design~\cite{steinberg1996vst}. A synthesizer consists of a series of digital signal processing (DSP) functions that transform a set of control parameters into audio outputs~\cite{stranneby2004digital}. In practice, sound designers iteratively manipulate these parameters until the synthesizer produces a desired sound~\cite{krekovic2019insights}. While this manual process can be a rewarding creative outlet, it requires expertise, patience, and significant time investment.

\textit{Sound-matching} refers to the automation of this workflow: given a target sound and a synthesizer, the objective is to identify parameter settings that produce a perceptually similar output~\cite{justice1979analytic,horner1993machine,mitchell2007evolutionary}. Sound-matching experiments may be conducted in \textit{in-domain} or \textit{out-of-domain} (OOD) settings. In in-domain experiments, the target sound is generated by the same synthesizer being optimized and the ground-truth parameters are known. In OOD settings, the target sound originates from a different synthesis process or recording, and exact replication is generally impossible. Despite the practical relevance of OOD sound-matching, prior work overwhelmingly focuses on in-domain evaluations~\cite{horner1993machine,yee2018automatic,han2023perceptual,vahidi2023mesostructures,salimi2025evaluating}.

Our recent work identified two structural issues in the field: the absence of a universally optimal loss function and the scarcity of systematic OOD evaluation~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}. In-domain experiments revealed that loss-function performance is strongly dependent on synthesis method; whether such synthesis-dependent behavior persists under OOD conditions remains an open question. The difficulty of OOD experimentation is exacerbated by standard automatic performance measures used for in-domain experiments (notably parameter recovery, or P-Loss) not being available OOD. In addition, large scale manual listening tests are practically impossible. Here we present a novel approach to OOD experimentation and use it to test the synthesizer-loss function dependence.

We propose a methodology for conducting controlled OOD experiments by designing synthesizer pairs with partially overlapping functionality, and introduce Partial Parameter Loss (PPL) as an automatic evaluation metric that operates within this framework. PPL measures the distance across the critical subset of parameters---the selection of which requires domain knowledge---shared between the target and imitator synthesizers, enabling automatic assessment of whether a loss function achieves a specific synthesis task in settings where full parameter ground truth does not exist. With this evaluation tool in hand, we test the hypothesis that the synthesis-dependent behavior of loss functions observed within in-domain sound-matching persists under OOD conditions, and that alignment with human perception requires domain-aware selection of similarity measures rather than universal metrics.

The central research questions addressed in this work are: \textbf{(Q1)} Does synthesis-dependent loss behavior generalize beyond in-domain settings? \textbf{(Q2)} Do the reported advantages and limitations of common loss functions persist under synthesizer mismatch? \textbf{(Q3)} Can PPL provide sufficient automatic evaluation to enable controlled OOD experiments, and how well do its results align with human perception?


\textbf{Contributions.} 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We demonstrate that loss-function effectiveness depends on method of synthesis in OOD settings, confirming that this pattern is not isolated to in-domain evaluation. Different similarity measures excel in filtering, amplitude-modulation, and pitch-modulation scenarios. This finding has direct practical consequences: the guidance developed from in-domain experiments transfers to OOD settings, and loss function selection should be informed by the synthesis task at hand.

    \item  We propose a methodology for conducting controlled OOD sound-matching experiments by designing synthesizer pairs with partially overlapping functionality. This provides the first systematic framework for OOD evaluation in sound-matching, addressing a gap where OOD experiments have been largely avoided despite their importance for verifying loss function performance.
    
    \item We introduce Partial Parameter Loss (PPL) as the automated evaluation metric within this framework. PPL measures task-specific loss function performance---whether a loss function recovers the critical parameters of interest---rather than attempting to replace holistic perceptual evaluation. We validate PPL against blinded human listening tests and show its utility as a diagnostic tool for identifying loss function capabilities.

\end{enumerate}

Together, these contributions address a significant gap in the sound-matching literature. OOD experiments have been largely avoided despite their critical importance for verifying loss function performance beyond idealized in-domain settings. By providing both the evaluation methodology and the empirical evidence that synthesis-dependent behavior persists under OOD conditions, this work offers actionable guidance for practitioners and a replicable framework for future OOD research.


\section{Background and Previous Work}
We begin with a review of related concepts of audio similarity measures, loss functions, and performance measures, then define the sound-matching problem, its subtypes, and the importance of out-of-domain experiments.

\subsection{Measuring Audio Similarity}
\label{sec:measuring_audio_similarity}
Manual sound-design often takes the form of a feedback loop where the designer hears an output and measures the similarity of what is currently generated versus what they would like to hear. This subjective, internal measurement of sound qualities is difficult to replicate between people with similar tastes, let alone digitally. 

Fourier based measurements of audio similarity are the go-to functions for representation and comparison of sounds~\cite{smith2007mathematics}. Fourier transformations extract the strength of frequencies present in a signal---the range and resolution of frequencies detected is dependent on the sampling rate and length of the signal. Short-time Fourier Transform (STFT) is perhaps the most common application, where Fourier transformation is applied to segments of the signal~\cite{smith2007mathematics}. Despite their ubiquity, there are many documented issues with the application of distance metrics on Fourier based losses~\cite{turian2020sorry,salimi2025evaluating,vahidi2023mesostructures}.

Joint-Time Frequency Scattering is the application of wavelet transformations to a time-frequency representation of sounds. It has been recently proposed as an improvement to Fourier-based losses when measuring \textit{mesostructures} of sounds---that is, anything between microstructures of waveshapes to macrostructures of musical arrangements~\cite{lostanlen2019shape}. In a recent work, we've argued that the capabilities of this loss function are likely to be much narrower than speculated~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}.

Parameter Loss (P-Loss) is another important measure of audio similarity~\cite{yee2018automatic,esling2019flow,masuda2021soundmatch}. P-Loss requires that the synthesizers making the two sounds have identical parameters. It is computed as the distance between normalized parameter vectors:

\begin{equation}
\text{P-Loss} = \|\theta - \theta_0\|_1.
\end{equation}


Audio similarity measures can be used as loss functions or performance measures; however, the two terms are given to distinct functions in the sound-matching framework, which we will discuss in the next section. 

\subsection{Sound-Matching Formulation}

Sound-matching can be formalized as an optimization problem. Given a parametric synthesizer, a target sound, and a similarity measure, the objective is to find synthesizer parameters that minimize the distance between the output and the target. The key components are:

\begin{itemize}
\item $g(\theta)$: A parametric audio synthesizer with parameters $\theta$ producing output $x_{\theta} = g(\theta)$.
\item $g^{t}(\theta^{t})$: The target synthesizer producing the target sound $x^{t} = g^{t}(\theta^{t})$. OOD conditions require that $g$ and $g^{t}$ differ in structure or parameter ranges such that their output sets do not overlap.
\item $\phi(\cdot)$: A feature representation function mapping waveforms into a comparison space.
\end{itemize}

The loss function is defined as
\[
L(\theta, x^{t}) = d(\phi(x_{\theta}), \phi(x^{t})),
\]
where $d$ denotes a distance metric. In iterative sound-matching, the parameters are randomly initialized and repeatedly updated using gradients of $L$ with respect to $\theta$ until convergence. This mirrors the manual workflow of sound designers, who listen, compare, adjust, and repeat. 

Sound-matching experiments are either \emph{in-domain} or \emph{out-of-domain} (OOD). In in-domain experiments, the target sound is generated by the same synthesizer being optimized ($g = g^{t}$), ground-truth parameters are known, and exact replication is achievable. In OOD experiments, the target originates from a different synthesizer or recording. Exact replication is generally impossible, and the optimization problem becomes one of imitation rather than replication. OOD settings introduce realistic challenges that are absent with in-domain settings: parameter ground truth does not exist (making standard P-Loss undefined), the imitator (by definition) lacks the ability to fully reproduce all features of the target, and there is a degree of subjectivity in determining which features should be imitated and how success should be measured. 

Despite the practical importance of OOD sound-matching, systematic OOD experiments are largely absent from the literature. Most works validate loss functions exclusively in in-domain settings, where the target sound is guaranteed to be reachable. This leaves open whether reported loss function advantages hold when the synthesizer cannot replicate the target---a condition that applies to nearly all real-world sound-design tasks. Furthermore, no established methodology exists for designing controlled OOD experiments or for automatic evaluation in the absence of full parameter ground truth. The following methodology addresses both gaps: we propose a framework for OOD experimentation based on synthesizer pairs with partially overlapping functionality, introduce Partial Parameter Loss as a diagnostic metric for verifying loss function capabilities, and evaluate four loss functions across seven carefully designed scenarios validated against human listening tests.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
\subsection{Problem Setup}
We evaluate four differentiable loss functions across five out-of-domain (OOD) and two in-domain sound-matching scenarios. The experimental structure is organized hierarchically:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{scenario} is defined by a pair of synthesizers---an imitator $g$ and a target $g^t$---modeling a specific type of synthesis mismatch (e.g., filtering, amplitude modulation, pitch-bending). Each scenario represents a distinct sound-design task.
    
    \item Within each scenario, four \textbf{experiments} are conducted, one for each loss function.
    
    \item Each experiment consists of \textbf{300 independent trials}. In each trial, a target sound is generated by randomly sampling parameters $\theta^t$ for $g^t$, the imitator parameters $\hat{\theta}$ are randomly initialized, and 200 steps of gradient-based optimization are performed to minimize the loss between $g(\hat{\theta})$ and $x^t = g^t(\theta^t)$.
\end{enumerate}

This yields 300 automatically assigned trial scores per loss function per scenario, enabling robust statistical comparison. Additionally, hearing tests are conducted for verification of results. All synthesizers produce 1-second audio signals at a sampling rate of 48{,}000~Hz.

\subsection{Loss Functions}
\label{subsec:loss_functions}
In Section~\ref{sec:measuring_audio_similarity}, we discussed the conceptual descriptions of four loss functions used in the previous in-domain study~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}. The same loss functions are used in this work as well. The implementation details relevant to reproducibility are provided here.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{\LoneSpec:}
Pointwise L1 distance between log-magnitude STFT spectrograms:
\begin{equation}
    L_{\mathrm{L1}}(\theta, x^{t}) 
    = \left\| STFT (x_{\theta}) - STFT (x^{t}) \right\|_{1}
\end{equation}



\medskip
\noindent\textbf{\SIMSESpec:}
The scale-invariant mean-squared error (SIMSE). This loss is identical to \LoneSpec, with the exception that it utilizes the SIMSE difference instead of L1.
\begin{equation}
    L_{\mathrm{SIMSE}}(\theta, x^{t}) 
    =  SIMSE( STFT (x_{\theta}) - STFT (x^{t}))
\end{equation}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{\JTFS:}
The joint time-frequency scattering (JTFS) transform provides a multiresolution representation of temporal and spectral structures of time-series~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures,anden2015joint}. The loss is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    L_{\mathrm{JTFS}}(\theta, x^{t})
    = 
    \left\|
        \Phi_{\mathrm{JTFS}}(x_{\theta})
        -
        \Phi_{\mathrm{JTFS}}(x^{t})
    \right\|_{2}
\end{equation}
Where L2 distance metric is applied to the 1-Dimensional JTFS transform provided by Andreux \textit{et. al}~\cite{kymatio,salimi2025evaluating}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{\DTWEnv:}
This loss computes a dynamic time-warping (DTW) distance between amplitude envelopes. Since the original DTW function is not differentiable, the soft-DTW distance is used instead~\cite{softdtw_jax,cuturi2017soft}. The envelope of the sound is calculated by taking the STFT, and summing the values of all frequency bins at every time-step.

\begin{equation}
    L_{\mathrm{DTW}}(\theta, x^{t})
    =
    \mathrm{DTW}
    \left(
        \mathrm{Env}(x_{\theta}),
        \mathrm{Env}(x^{t})
    \right).
\end{equation}


For all STFT-based computations (\LoneSpec, \SIMSESpec, and the envelope extraction for \DTWEnv), we use 512 FFT bins, a window size of 600 samples, and a hop length of 100 samples, consistent with previous work~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}.


\subsection{Evaluation Methods}
\label{subsec:evaluation}
Each OOD experiment is evaluated using two complementary methods: the automatic Partial Parameter Loss (PPL), which we introduce shortly, and blinded human listening tests. The procedures for statistical comparison of the resulting scores are described in Section~\ref{sec:bootstrapping_ranking}.

 % Because standard P-Loss is undefined when the target and imitator synthesizers have different architectures, OOD evaluation requires an alternative automatic metric. We introduce Partial Parameter Loss for this purpose and validate it against blinded human listening tests. PPL is not intended as a surrogate for holistic perceptual similarity; rather, it measures whether a loss function achieves a specific, well-defined synthesis taskm, that is, recovering the critical parameters identified by the experimenter. The two evaluation methods described below are therefore complementary: PPL provides scalable, automatic diagnosis of loss function capabilities, while hearing tests provide holistic perceptual ground truth.

\subsubsection{Partial P-Loss}
\label{sec:partial_ploss}
In in-domain sound-matching, Parameter Loss (P-Loss) measures the distance between the full parameter vectors of the target and output synthesizers. In OOD settings, the target and imitator synthesizers have different architectures, making full parameter comparison undefined. To address this, we propose \emph{Partial Parameter Loss} (PPL), which measures the distance only across the subset of parameters that have perceptual correspondence between the two synthesizers.

PPL measures the L2 distance only across the subset of parameters considered perceptually relevant for the scenario:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{PPL} = \| \theta^{(\mathrm{crit})}_t - \hat{\theta}^{(\mathrm{crit})} \|_2,
\end{equation}
Here $\theta^{(\mathrm{crit})}_t$ and $\hat{\theta}^{(\mathrm{crit})}$ denote the corresponding parameters of interest between $g$ and $g^t$.  All critical parameters are normalized to the range [0, 1] based on their valid ranges as defined in the synthesizer program before computing PPL.

In OOD scenarios, some level of subjectivity is unavoidable in selecting such critical parameters. However, the scenarios here have been designed with a focus on a single synthesis task in order to make the PPL definition fairly straightforward. With careful design, PPL can be a reasonable interpretable comparison across synthesizers. The exact PPL parameters used for each scenario will be discussed in their corresponding subsection later on in Section~\ref{sec:scenarios}, and summarized in Table~\ref{tab:scenario_overview}.

\subsubsection{Hearing tests}
Listening tests serve as the primary ground truth for evaluating perceptual similarity. For each scenario, we randomly sample 40 sound-pairs (target and imitator output) for each of the four loss functions, yielding 160 sound-pairs per scenario. Two of the authors independently evaluated these samples in a blinded manner, that is, the loss functions used in the trial were not disclosed.

Each listener assigned a Likert score from 1 (not similar at all) to 5 (near identical) to each sound-pair. No practice trials were conducted, though both listeners had prior experience with the evaluation protocol from the in-domain study~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}. The scores from both listeners were combined, giving 80 evaluation scores per loss function per scenario (compared to 300 for PPL), which are then bootstrapped for statistical comparison as described in Section 3.5.


\subsection{Optimization Loop}
\label{subsec:optimization}

All synthesizers are implemented in Faust and transpiled into differentiable JAX functions using DawDreamer~\cite{braun2024dac}. This pipeline allows synthesizer programs to be defined in a high-level DSP language while enabling gradient-based optimization through automatic differentiation. In each trial, the imitator synthesizer parameters $\hat{\theta}$ are initialized by uniform random sampling over the parameter ranges defined in the Faust program. The target parameters $\theta^t$ are similarly sampled. Parameters are updated using the RMSProp optimizer with a fixed learning rate of 0.045, consistent with previous work~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}. The maximum number of optimization steps is 200; based on our observations, parameters have either converged to a local minimum or diverged beyond recovery by this point.

Gradient clipping is applied to ensure that the L2 norm of all gradients does not exceed a threshold of 1, following the same protocol as the in-domain study to mitigate exploding gradients in the sequential signal processing chain. Furthermore, recent work has shown that gradient clipping can improve neural network optimization for perceptual sound-matching~\cite{han2025gradient}. 

\subsection{Bootstrapping and Ranking Procedures}
\label{sec:bootstrapping_ranking}

To compare loss function performance within each scenario, we construct bootstrapped distributions of the evaluation scores. For each loss function, the 300 PPL scores (or 80 Likert scores) are resampled with replacement at 100\% of the original sample size, and the mean of each resample is computed. This process is repeated 1,000 times, yielding an empirical distribution of the mean performance that avoids parametric assumptions about the underlying score distribution~\cite{chernick2011bootstrap}.

For each performance measure and scenario, nonparametric Scott-Knott procedure (NPSK) recursively partitions the bootstrapped distribution to maximize between-group separation while minimizing within-group variance, producing statistically justified ranks without requiring normality~\cite{tantithamthavorn2017mvt,tantithamthavorn2018optimization}. Rank~1 corresponds to the best-performing distribution, with larger ranks indicating worse performance. Ties occur when two or more distributions cannot be statistically distinguished under the NPSK splitting criterion, resulting in equal assigned ranks.

\subsection{AM-Synthesizer Matching}
\label{sec:am_sound_matching}

 This group of scenarios contains three OOD scenarios built from two AM synthesizers.  \FMMod and \FMModvtwo as described in Listings~\ref{lst:program3} and~\ref{lst:program3_v2} are the basis for the three variations of this scenario. \FMMod generates a sound by modifying the amplitude of a saw oscillator with a sinusoidal LFO. \FMModvtwo uses sine oscillators for both the carrier and the modulator. In this section, we will refer to the LFO frequency as \texttt{amp} and carrier frequency as \texttt{car}. The value of \texttt{amp} shapes the amplitude of the sound (or the wobbling effect), and \texttt{car} determines the frequency (or pitch) of the sound.

In these scenarios, the \texttt{amp} values can match, but the frequency content cannot. In version 1 (Non-Overlapping Frequencies), the range of available frequencies are different. In version 2 (Sine Target, Saw Imitator), the imitator can only make sine tones while the target can only makes saw waves, and vice versa for scenario 3 (Saw Target, Sine Imitator). A question we have to consider is: how would we match the sounds manually? Here we see why OOD experiments are difficult, as describing sound similarity can be quite subjective.


\textbf{PPL definition for all AM scenarios.} Across all three AM variants, PPL is defined as the squared difference between the \texttt{amp} values of the target and imitator. The \texttt{amp}  parameter is meaningful across synthesizers because the amplitude modulation rate transfers perceptually regardless of the carrier waveform or frequency range. 

\textbf{Why exclude the carrier frequency?} In OOD settings where the carrier frequencies cannot match exactly, the ``correct'' frequency is ambiguous. Consider a target producing 440 Hz (A4 in equal temperament) and an imitator limited to frequencies below 400 Hz. The closest numerical value is 400 Hz, but musically, 220 Hz (A3, one octave below) may be a better perceptual match than 392 Hz (G4, a different note). More generally, given the logarithmic nature of pitch perception, the best frequency matches for a given frequency $f$ are arguably $f \cdot 2^n$ for integer $n$~\cite{young1939terminology,sethares2005tuning}. Because there is no single correct definition of carrier frequency similarity in the OOD setting, we exclude it from PPL and focus on the amplitude modulation rate, which is both unambiguous and a common transfer target in practical sound design~\cite{engel2020ddsp}.


% If the carrier frequencies could not possibly match (as in scenario 1), the best method for matching the carrier frequencies is not clear. Although the distances between the frequencies can be reduced, there will always be a gap. What's more, matching musical notes is not simply a matter of frequency values. For example, let's consider the commonly used equal temperament tuning system~\cite{sethares2005tuning}, where the A4 note is usually associated with 440 Hz. In this system, if the target synth is producing a frequency at 440 Hz, and the imitator can only produce values below 400 Hz, then the correct objective for the frequency is dependent on the sound designers needs. 400Hz might be the best frequency parameter, since it is as close as we can get to 440Hz; or perhaps the value of 220 Hz (corresponding to A3) is a better match than the value of 392 Hz (corresponding to G4). In general, considering the commonly used logarithmic scaling of musical notes, it can be argued that the best matches for any frequency $f$ are $f*2^{n}$, where n is any integer~\cite{young1939terminology}. \todo{(does this tie back into the hypothesis that the sinusoidal nature of sound can cause non-smooth loss functions)}

To measure sound matching success, the PPL is set to the squared difference between the amp values in $\theta_t$ and the output parameters.

\subsubsection{Non-Overlapping frequencies}
\label{sec:am_sound_matching_nonoverlapping}
\textbf{Setup.} For this scenario, we choose two instances of \FMModvtwo, with the carriers having non-overlapping frequency ranges of 30-250 Hz for the target synth and 1000-5000 Hz for the imitator. Here, the carrier is a sine oscillator, therefore the clashing of higher harmonics can be factored out as a source of confusion in the loss function landscape. Since there is no overlap in the carrier frequency ranges, the tone of the imitator and target synth can never match, making this an OOD scenario. 

\noindent \textbf{Results.}
\DTWEnv is the best performer under both PPL and hearing tests. This is consistent with \DTWEnv's focus on amplitude envelope dynamics: since the carrier frequencies cannot match, the modulation rate is the only transferable feature, and \DTWEnv captures it most effectively. \JTFS is the worst performer in both measures, suggesting that its spectro-temporal representation is confounded by the large frequency gap between target and imitator.



\subsubsection{Sine Target, Saw Imitator}
\label{sec:am_sinetarget_sawimitate}
\textbf{Setup.} In this scenario, we select \FMModvtwo as the target, and \FMMod as the imitator, both with \texttt{amp} ranges of 1-15 Hz and \texttt{car} range of 30-5000 Hz. Unlike the non-overlapping scenario, the carrier frequencies can overlap numerically. However, the harmonic content differs: a sine carrier produces a pure tone, while a saw carrier produces a harmonic series.

\noindent \textbf{Results.} \DTWEnv again achieves the best PPL score, confirming its ability to recover the correct modulation rate. However, hearing tests favor the spectrogram-based losses (\SIMSESpec ranks first). The likely explanation is that \DTWEnv  matches the amplitude modulation accurately but ignores harmonic content, while spectrogram losses produce outputs with better overall timbral similarity at the cost of envelope accuracy. This scenario highlights the divergence between PPL (which measures a specific parameter) and holistic perceptual judgment in human listening.


\subsubsection{Saw Target, Sine Imitator}
\label{sec:am_sawtarget_sineimitate}
\textbf{Setup.} This scenario uses \FMMod as the target and \FMModvtwo as the imitator. The parameter ranges remain unchanged. 

\noindent \textbf{Results.}  The pattern is similar to the previous scenario, further supporting the interpretation that \DTWEnv excels at amplitude modulation, but produces outputs that listeners rate lower when harmonic content is mismatched.


\subsection{Pitch-Bending}
\label{sec:chirplet_scenarios}
This group tests three scenarios involving synthesizers that produce tones with increasing pitch, or ``chirplets''~\cite{mann1995chirplet,lostanlen2019shape}. The main scenario (Section 4.3.1) is OOD, while the remaining two (Sections 4.3.2 and 4.3.3) are in-domain variants included for comparison with prior work by Vahidi et al.\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures}. 

The base synthesizer (Listing~\ref{lst:pitchbendup}) produces a sine wave at a starting pitch that remains steady for a delay period, after which an exponential upward pitch bend is applied. The search parameters are the starting pitch (30â€“500 Hz) and the rate of pitch increase (1â€“20 samples per time step).


\subsubsection{Delayed Pitch-Bending}
\label{sec:delayed_chirplet}
\textbf{Setup.} The program here is a sine wave with a steady starting pitch for a random amount of time; after a time delay of 5000-30000 samples, an exponential upward pitch bend is applied to the starting pitch. The time-delay for the pitch-bending onset is selected randomly for each experiment, so it is very unlikely that the imitator would be able to replicate the target's sounds, making this an OOD scenario. Here, we can define success with PPL (the delay parameter is not adjusted during search), that is, how well the \texttt{increase\_speed} and \texttt{starting\_pitch} parameters are approximated despite the delay in the pitch-bending onset, using L2 distance. 

\noindent \textbf{Results.} This scenario shows identical ratings between both PPL and hearing tests. Both measures show \JTFS as the best performing loss. This is consistent with the findings by Vahidi et al. which showed \JTFS to be agnostic to slight perturbations in the frequency and time scale when matching chirplet synthesizers (a feature that strongly penalizes spectrogram-based losses~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures}. 


\subsubsection{No-Delay Pitch-Bending}
\label{sec:no_delayed_chirplet}
\textbf{Setup.} As a follow-up experiment, we conducted the \textit{in-domain} comparison of pitch-bending synthesizers \textit{without} delay. This scenario would be a close replication of the original in-domain experiments by Vahidi \textit{et. al.}. In other words, it is identical to the delayed pitch-bending experiment but the delay variable $\delta$ is always set to 0 (see Listing~\ref{lst:pitchbendup}).  

\noindent \textbf{Results.} \JTFS remains the best performer under both PPL and hearing tests, consistent with the OOD delayed scenario and with prior work. 

\subsubsection{Pulsating Pitch-Bending}
\label{sec:pulsating_chirplet}
\textbf{Setup.} This scenario modifies the pitch-bending synthesizer to include a pulsating component (Listing~\ref{lst:pitchbendup_pulse}). The frequency increases overall but also oscillates up and down within a narrow range, driven by a saw-wave LFO with frequency \texttt{pulse\_rate}. The search parameters are \texttt{increase\_speed} and \texttt{pulse\_rate}. This is an in-domain experiment included to test whether \JTFS's advantage extends to pitch trajectories with added modulation --- a feature that falls within the definition of ``mesostructure'' as described by Vahidi et al~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures}. PPL is defined as the L2 distance between the normalized \texttt{increase\_speed} and \texttt{pulse\_rate} parameters.

\noindent \textbf{Results.} \DTWEnv ranks first under both PPL and hearing tests, displacing \JTFS from the top rank it held in the other two chirplet scenarios. \JTFS drops to second place. This result is significant because the pulsating pitch trajectory falls under the definition of mesostructure and represents only a slight modification to the original chirplet experiments. This suggests that \JTFS's strength is more narrowly scoped than the ``mesostructure'' framing implies.

\subsection{Summary of Results}

Table~\ref{tab:scenario_ranks} presents the full ranking results across all seven scenarios. A few patterns can be observed:

No single loss function dominates. \SIMSESpec{} is the top performer for band-pass matching, \DTWEnv{} for amplitude modulation and pulsating pitch-bending, and \JTFS{} for chirplet-like pitch trajectories. Each loss function ranks first in at least one scenario.

The agreement between parameter based losses and hearing tests is strong but imperfect. The two measures assign identical top ranks in five of seven scenarios. The two disagreements occur in the AM scenarios with overlapping carrier frequencies (Sections~\ref{sec:am_sinetarget_sawimitate} and~\ref{sec:am_sawtarget_sineimitate}), where PPL favors \DTWEnv{} for its accurate recovery of the modulation rate while listeners favor spectrogram-based losses for their better overall timbral similarity.

\LoneSpec{}, despite being the most commonly used spectrogram loss in the literature, does not rank first in any scenario under hearing tests. \SIMSESpec{} consistently matches or outperforms it, suggesting that scale-invariant comparison may be an effective approach to spectrogram-based loss computation.

\subsubsection{Inter-Rater Agreement}

Moderate to strong correlation was observed between the two listeners' Likert scores, measured using Spearman's rank correlation~\cite{spearman1987proof}. Table~\ref{tab:listener_correlation} shows the per-scenario and overall correlation coefficients. The overall correlation was $\rho = 0.59$ ($p < 10^{-107}$), with per-scenario values ranging from $\rho = 0.36$ (sine-to-saw AM) to $\rho = 0.67$ (delayed pitch-bending).

This is notably lower than the $\rho = 0.86$ reported for in-domain evaluation in~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}. The decline is expected: in in-domain settings, good results sound nearly identical to the target and poor results sound clearly wrong, making similarity judgments straightforward. In OOD settings, exact replication is impossible, so listeners must judge degrees of partial similarity. Different listeners may weigh different features---one may prioritize pitch accuracy while another attends more to timbral similarity or rhythmic feel. Despite the lower overall agreement, all correlations are statistically significant, and this level of consistency is sufficient to treat the combined listener scores as a meaningful ground truth for ranking purposes.

\section{Discussion}
Based on the experiment results, we can now provide answers for our research questions as well as some practical implications.

\subsection{Key Findings}
\textbf{Q1:} Does Synthesis-Dependent Loss Behavior Generalize Beyond In-Domain Settings? \\ Yes. Across all seven scenarios, the relative effectiveness of loss functions remained tied to the synthesis task rather than converging on a single best performer. \SIMSESpec{} dominated band-pass filtering, \DTWEnv{} dominated amplitude modulation, and \JTFS{} dominated smooth pitch trajectories---the same pattern observed in-domain~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}.

This finding has a direct consequence for practitioners: the choice of loss function should be informed by the synthesis task at hand, not selected once and applied universally. The guidance developed from in-domain experiments in~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}---spectrogram losses for filtering, \DTWEnv{} for amplitude modulation, and \JTFS{} for chirplets---transfers to OOD settings.

\textbf{Q2:} Do Reported Advantages and Limitations Persist Under Synthesizer Mismatch?\\
Largely, yes, however, certain caveats must be noted. \SIMSESpec{} retained its advantage over \LoneSpec{} for band-pass matching, consistent with the in-domain findings, highlighting the benefit of scale-invariant comparison when filter cutoffs reshape spectral energy without predictable amplitude changes. Notably, \LoneSpec{}, despite being widely used in the literature, never ranked first under hearing tests. \DTWEnv{} remained the strongest performer for amplitude modulation tasks: in the non-overlapping frequency scenario, where carrier frequencies could not match, it dominated both PPL and hearing tests because envelope dynamics were the only transferable feature. However, in AM scenarios with overlapping carrier frequencies, a limitation emerged: while \DTWEnv{} accurately recovered the modulation rate and achieved the best PPL scores, listeners preferred spectrogram-based losses that better preserved overall timbral similarity. This divergence illustrates how OOD conditions introduce a trade-off between matching specific signal features and transferring a broad spectrum of sound characteristics. Finally, \JTFS{} retained its advantage for chirplet-like pitch trajectories, ranking first in both delayed (OOD) and no-delay (in-domain) pitch-bending scenarios; in particular, the delayed case shows how \JTFS{}'s multi-resolution timeâ€“frequency representation accommodates temporal offsets that strongly penalize spectrogram-based losses, confirming the robustness reported by Vahidi et~al.~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures}.

However, the pulsating pitch-bend scenario challenges broader claims about \JTFS{}. The pulsating pitch trajectory---a pitch that increases overall while oscillating periodically---falls within the definition of ``mesostructure'' given in~\cite{vahidi2023mesostructures}, yet \DTWEnv{} outperformed \JTFS{} under both PPL and hearing tests. Combined with the AM results, this suggests that the effectiveness of \JTFS{} may be more narrowly scoped than the ``mesostructure'' label implies. A more precise characterization is that \JTFS{} excels at capturing smooth spectro-temporal trajectories, whereas \DTWEnv{} (or other DTW based losses) is better suited to periodic modulation patterns, whether they modulate amplitude or pitch.

\textbf{Q3:} Can PPL Serve as a Reliable Automated Metric?\\
PPL is not designed as a substitute for human perception of sound. Rather, it measures whether a loss function achieves a specific, well-defined synthesis task---recovering the critical parameters that the experimenter has identified as the target of the sound-matching process. If designed properly, PPL provides consistent and informative results regarding the success and utility of loss functions.

PPL is most reliable as a standalone metric when the critical parameters capture the perceptually dominant feature of the scenario. This held in three of five OOD scenarios (band-pass, AM non-overlap, delayed chirp), where PPL and hearing tests agreed on the top-ranked loss. In the remaining two OOD scenarios (AM with overlapping carriers), additional perceptual dimensions competed for listener attention, causing the two measures to diverge. The pattern is predictable: when the synthesis task of interest aligns closely with the critical parameters, PPL provides a scalable evaluation method; when multiple perceptual features compete, PPL cannot replace hearing tests.

PPL can be utilized in scalable, automatic evaluation of loss function capabilities in OOD settings. However, we recommend validating PPL against a smaller set of listening tests before use in large-scale evaluation, particularly when the choice of critical parameters involves judgment about which features are perceptually dominant.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

The findings of this work lead to several practical recommendations for both practitioners and researchers.

\textbf{Loss function selection is a creative decision.} There is no universally best loss function. Instead, the choice should be guided by the synthesis task: spectrogram-based losses (preferably \SIMSESpec{} over \LoneSpec{}) for filtering tasks, \DTWEnv{} for amplitude modulation tasks, and \JTFS{} for smooth pitch trajectory tasks. Much like selecting a synthesizer for a desired sound, the loss function should be considered part of the creative toolkit.

\textbf{SIMSE deserves wider adoption.} \SIMSESpec{} matched or outperformed \LoneSpec{} in every scenario tested across both this work and the in-domain study~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}. The common practice of applying L1 or L2 distances to spectrograms may therefore be suboptimal, particularly in settings where global gain changes accompany the synthesis process.

\textbf{Specialized losses can outperform general-purpose metrics.} Both \DTWEnv{} and \SIMSESpec{} outperform more established approaches such as \JTFS{} and \LoneSpec{} when applied to tasks aligned with their design. This aligns with the takeaway from the in-domain experiments of past work~\cite{salimi2025evaluating}, suggesting that developing additional loss functions targeting specific perceptual dimensions (e.g., roughness, spectral centroid trajectories, or onset characteristics) may be more productive than pursuing a single universal metric. 

\textbf{If applicable, PPL can be a viable strategy for OOD evaluation.} For researchers constructing OOD benchmarks with overlapping synthesizers, PPL is most effective when the chosen critical parameters are both shared across synthesizers and perceptually dominant in the resulting sound. When the synthesis task of interest aligns with the critical parameters, PPL provides a scalable evaluation method. In practice, validating PPL against a smaller set of listening tests before use in large-scale evaluation is recommended.


\section{Conclusion, Limitations, and Future Work}

\textbf{Summary:} This work addressed a significant gap in the sound-matching literature: the near-total absence of systematic OOD evaluation. While OOD conditions---where the target sound is unreachable---reflect the reality of practical sound design, prior work has overwhelmingly validated loss functions only in idealized in-domain settings. We proposed a methodology for conducting controlled OOD experiments by designing synthesizer pairs with partially overlapping functionality, enabling both the creation of realistic synthesis-mismatch scenarios and the automatic evaluation of results through Partial Parameter Loss (PPL).

Across seven scenarios involving band-pass filtering, amplitude modulation, and pitch-bending synthesizers, we evaluated four differentiable loss functions and confirmed that their effectiveness remains tightly coupled to the method of synthesis in OOD scenarios. PPL proved useful as a diagnostic tool for verifying loss function capabilities: it identifies what each loss is good at (e.g., \DTWEnv for amplitude modulation, \SIMSESpec for filter matching, \JTFS for pitch trajectories) rather than attempting to replace manual, perceptual evaluations. Parameter-based metrics agreed with hearing tests in five of seven cases overall; PPL specifically agreed in three of five OOD scenarios, with disagreements occurring predictably when perceptually important features fell outside the critical parameter set. We also observed lower inter-rater agreement in OOD evaluations compared to previous in-domain results ($\rho$ = 0.59 versus 0.86), reflecting the inherent subjectivity of judging similarity when exact replication is impossible.

\textbf{Weaknesses:} Several limitations should be noted. The scenarios here, while carefully designed, use relatively simple synthesizer pairs with few parameters. Whether PPL remains a useful metric for more complex synthesizers with higher-dimensional parameter spaces is an open question. The definition of "critical parameters" in PPL requires domain knowledge and judgment, introducing a degree of subjectivity into what is otherwise an automatic measure. Additionally, all target sounds in this work originate from other synthesizers rather than from real-world recordings, which would introduce further challenges such as noise, room acoustics, and timbral complexity beyond the reach of simple DSP models.

\textbf{Future Work:} The methodology proposed here---designing synthesizer pairs with controlled partial overlap---is intended as a starting point for OOD evaluation, not a final solution. Sound-matching is not practical until successful OOD evaluations with pre-recorded targets (regardless of source) are achieved, where neither parameter ground truth nor structural correspondence exists. We hope that the approach demonstrated here lowers the barrier to conducting OOD experiments, encouraging the field to move beyond in-domain validation as the default.  Multi-objective loss combinations that leverage the complementary strengths observed here---for example, pairing \DTWEnv with a spectrogram loss to capture both envelope dynamics and harmonic content---are a promising direction. The periodic and multi-modal nature of loss landscapes in sound-matching also remains an open problem; adaptive optimizers or reinforcement learning approaches may offer improvements over fixed-rate gradient descent. Finally, expanding the set of differentiable loss functions to target other (at times subjective) perceptual dimensions---such as roughness, brightness, or spatial characteristics---could bring sound-matching closer to the nuanced judgments and preferences of sound-designers.
